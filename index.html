Engineering-or-not Prediction Model

The people who I am basing this model is a combination of people I knew from my classes, my old high school and the running club I participate in during college.

Some people who are taking this survey are also random friends I met on campus with no affilation to my classes or the running club.

As a result there is a nice diversity of majors. However, there was not enough of a sample to predict all majors well.

As a result I predicted whether someone was in the college of engineering or not, because I had the most people from that department.

This model was extracted by a function, using the Akaike Information Criterion tool in R Programming. I created as simplified and accurate of a model as I could.

The criterion penalizes both having too many variables and too much of an error residual. Here is the model:

y = 0.21868 + 0.39413*x1 - 0.69162*x2 - 0.18771*x3 - 0.07564*x4 +0.13148*x5 + 0.33850*x6 - 0.23589*x7 + 0.05217*x8 - 0.17200*x9 + 0.11330*x10 0.21868 + 0.35753*x11 + 0.29785*x12 - 0.19925*x13 - 0.19962*x14 - 0.0.22983*x15

1 = Male, 2 = Gender not male or female, 3 = Loves Major, 4 = Stressed Out, 5 = Reads Sometimes, 6 = Bookworm, 7 = Tuition Paid Mostly by Financial Aid

8 = No Financial Aid, 9 = Partial Financial Aid, 10 = Virginity at 16 or 17, 11 = Minority, 12 = Likes Adventure Movies, 13 = Likes Action Movies, 14 = Does not Game, 15 = Games Sometimes

The reason this model includes a subset of all variables is because a lot of variables not included have covariance with the variables already in the model.
